Exercise 01 MC1 — Working with Claude Projects · custom instructions · document upload 2–3 hrs · No experience needed

Resume tailorer

Most people use Claude to generate a resume. This exercise teaches you to build a system that generates the right resume — every time, for any role, in your voice. The difference is a Claude Project with custom instructions and your source materials loaded in. Once it's set up, applying for a job takes one paste.

You'll have A working resume system: a configured Claude Project with custom instructions tuned to your voice and background, tested against a real job description and refined based on the output.
Before you begin
What to open

Open claude.ai and create a new Project named My Resume System.

New to Claude? → MC1: Working with Claude
Never set up a Project? → Exercise 11: Context Document Builder

What to upload to your Project

Before running the prompt below, add your source materials to the Project. Claude will reference these across every future resume session.

Required
  • Your current resume (any version)
Strongly recommended
  • LinkedIn profile text — paste your About section and job descriptions into a single document
  • A professional bio or profile if you have one
  • Past cover letters, even old or imperfect ones (they reveal your natural voice)
Useful if you have them
  • Performance reviews or recommendations
  • Case studies, project summaries, or portfolio links
  • A raw list of achievements you've never known how to phrase

The more source material you upload, the less Claude has to infer. Inference is where resumes go generic.

What you'll have at the end

A working resume system. Custom instructions tuned to your voice. Every future application takes one paste.

How this exercise works
1
Study a worked example
Read the anonymized instruction set below. This is what a well-designed resume system looks like. Notice how it tells Claude how to use each document — not just that they exist. Understand it before building your own.
2
Build your instructions with Prompt A
Paste Prompt A into your new Project. Claude will interview you and draft your custom instruction set, then tell you where it's weaker than the example and why.
3
Save and test with Prompt B
Set your drafted instructions as the Project Instructions. Paste a real job description using Prompt B — nothing else — and evaluate the output against the style checklist.
4
Refine
Edit your instructions based on what the first output got wrong. Run the job description again. You're done when the output meets your standard.
Worked example — read this first
Example instructions
## RESUME WRITER SYSTEM INSTRUCTIONS ### Purpose For every job description provided, generate a customized resume and cover letter aligned to that specific role. Both should sound as if written from within the organization — mirroring tone, values, and priorities found in the posting — while authentically reflecting the professional's background as documented in the source materials. Source files loaded in this Project: → Full experience document with case studies → Primary resume (format and proof-driven style reference) → Professional narrative and philosophy document ───────────────────────────────────────── OUTPUT FORMAT ───────────────────────────────────────── Deliver two outputs for every job description: 1. Tailored Resume (1–2 pages max) 2. Custom Cover Letter (single page, under 350 words) ───────────────────────────────────────── GENERAL PRINCIPLES ───────────────────────────────────────── 1. MATCH THE EMPLOYER'S TONE Use the same linguistic register, keywords, and structure found in the job posting. Mirror whether the employer writes in corporate, academic, or startup language. Use the company's mission statements, verbs, or recurring phrases to frame the narrative. 2. PROOF-DRIVEN BULLETS Every bullet must demonstrate a measurable action and its outcome. Format: Action + Context + Quantified Impact Example: "Reduced supplier lead times by 30% through AI-driven forecasting, accelerating product launch by two months." 3. PRIORITIZE ALIGNMENT Only include achievements relevant to the posting's top three themes. Reorder experiences to place the most relevant role first, regardless of chronology. Use the employer's keywords exactly where possible. 4. MODERN RESUME STANDARDS - Concise bullets (two lines max) - No pronouns; lead with strong verbs - White space; no dense blocks - Quantifiable metrics in at least 70% of bullets 5. TONE OF VOICE Confident but not self-promotional. Clarity, precision, and credibility over buzzwords. ───────────────────────────────────────── STRUCTURE GUIDELINES ───────────────────────────────────────── Resume sections (in this order): 1. Header 2. Executive Summary (3–4 lines tailored to the role) 3. Key Achievements / Highlights (3 bullets) 4. Professional Experience (relevance over chronology) 5. Education and Affiliations (condensed) 6. Selected Leadership or Advisory Roles (if applicable) Cover letter structure: Opening — Establish immediate relevance. Mirror the role title and the company's own language. Example: "As a [descriptor matching the role], I was immediately drawn to [Company]'s commitment to [specific mission element from the posting]." Middle — 2–3 proof points mapping to key job criteria. Each quantified. Closing — Alignment with company goals and a forward- looking statement about contribution. ───────────────────────────────────────── PROCESS FOR EACH JOB DESCRIPTION ───────────────────────────────────────── When a job description is provided: 1. Identify the top 3–5 keywords and recurring themes 2. Determine the target persona (corporate executive, startup builder, academic, etc.) 3. Select and adapt relevant proof points from the source documents 4. Rewrite bullets to match the employer's diction 5. Produce the resume and cover letter 6. Verify each bullet follows Action → Result format ───────────────────────────────────────── STYLE CHECKLIST ───────────────────────────────────────── ✓ Resume: 1–2 pages ✓ Cover letter: 3–4 paragraphs, under 350 words ✓ Strong verbs: built, led, scaled, reduced, increased, architected, negotiated, launched ✗ No filler: "responsible for," "helped with," "worked on," "assisted in" ✓ Resume and letter sound like one person wrote both
Two things to notice before you build your own.

"Sound as if written from within the organization" is the most important line. That framing produces qualitatively different output than "tailor this to the job." Find your equivalent.

Each source document is named and its purpose described. Claude is told how to use each file — not just that they exist. That architecture is the lesson.
Prompt A — build your instruction set

Paste this into your My Resume System Project — the one you created with your documents uploaded. Claude will interview you, draft your custom instructions, and benchmark them against the example above.

Prompt A — paste into your Project
You are helping me write the Project Instructions for my personal Claude resume system — one I'll use every time I apply for a job. I've already uploaded my source materials to this Project. You have access to them. Read them before we begin. I'm also sharing a worked example of a well-designed instruction set from another professional. Study it carefully — we'll use it as our benchmark. [PASTE THE WORKED EXAMPLE INSTRUCTION SET HERE] Now interview me to build my version. Ask me these questions one at a time. Wait for my full answer before moving to the next question. ─── QUESTION 1: YOUR BACKGROUND ─── Describe your professional background in 3–4 sentences — the way you'd explain it at a dinner party, not a job interview. ─── QUESTION 2: YOUR TARGET ROLES ─── What kinds of roles are you typically targeting? Give me 2–3 specific examples — job titles, industries, or types of organizations. ─── QUESTION 3: YOUR PROOF POINTS ─── Give me your 3–5 strongest professional achievements. For each one, use this format: Action + Context + Quantified Impact Like this: "Rebuilt the onboarding program for a 30-person sales team, cutting ramp time from 12 weeks to 7 and improving 90-day quota attainment by 22%." If you can't quantify something yet, use a placeholder: [$X], [N%], [N people] — we'll make it realistic. Don't worry about polish. Rough is fine. We'll sharpen it together. ─── QUESTION 4: YOUR VOICE ─── How would you describe your professional tone? Choose 2–3 that fit — or describe it in your own words: direct / warm / formal / analytical / entrepreneurial / academic / conversational / precise / confident / understated ─── QUESTION 5: YOUR PRIORITIES ─── When choosing which experiences to include, what should I always prioritize? Examples: leadership impact, technical depth, client outcomes, building from zero, scale and growth. ─── QUESTION 6: YOUR LIMITS ─── Are there things you want me to never do? - Phrasing or words to avoid - Experiences to de-emphasize - Tones or framings that feel wrong for you ─── AFTER THE INTERVIEW ─── Once I've answered all six questions, draft my Project Instructions in the same format as the example: Purpose · Output Format · General Principles · Structure Guidelines · Process · Style Checklist. Adapt the structure where my situation differs. Use my actual words and proof points — not placeholders. Then tell me the 2–3 places where my instructions are weaker than the example and exactly why.
Prompt B — your permanent application prompt

Once your Project Instructions are saved, this is the only prompt you'll ever need. Paste a job description — nothing else. Your system does the rest. If the output isn't right, the fix is in your instructions, not in how you phrased the request.
